Brewing

Match bean style to brew method

Pour-over

Choose light to medium roasts with floral or citrus notes. Use a medium grind and 1:16 ratio.

French press

Best for chocolatey, nutty coffees with heavier body. Use coarse grind and a 4-minute steep.

Espresso

Favor coffees with sweetness and structure. Dial in finer grind and tighter brew ratio.

Roasting

Quick guide for home roasters

Roasting your own coffee gives you more control over flavor and freshness. While it might seem complex at first, the basics are simple to understand and a great way to experiment with different profiles.

1. Start with Green (Unroasted) Beans

Unroasted beans are stable and last longer than roasted coffee. They also give you full control over how light or dark you want your roast.

2. Apply Heat Evenly

Roasting is all about applying consistent heat so the beans roast evenly. This can be done using a home roaster, stovetop method, or even an oven.

3. Watch for Key Stages

  • Drying Stage: Beans turn yellow and begin to lose moisture
  • First Crack: A popping sound—light roast stage begins
  • Second Crack: Darker roast with deeper, bolder flavors

4. Cool the Beans Quickly

Once your desired roast is reached, remove the beans from heat and cool them quickly to stop the roasting process.

5. Let Them Rest

Freshly roasted beans release gases for about 12–24 hours. Letting them rest improves flavor and brewing consistency.

Home Roasting Tips

  • Start with small batches to learn the process
  • Use good ventilation—roasting produces smoke
  • Keep beans moving for even roasting
  • Track time and temperature for consistency
  • Experiment with different roast levels

What You'll Need

  • Green coffee beans
  • Heat source (roaster, stovetop, or oven)
  • Stirring tool or rotating drum
  • Cooling tray or colander

FAQ

Common questions

Buy roasted if you want to brew right away. Buy unroasted if you roast at home and want more control over development.

Store your coffee in an airtight container in a cool, dry place. Avoid keeping it in the fridge or freezer, as moisture can affect flavor.

Roasted coffee is best within 2–4 weeks after opening. Unroasted beans last longer, typically several months when stored properly.
